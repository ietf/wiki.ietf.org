# Chinese Youth Talent Program

## Increased Student Participation at IETF 125 Through the Chinese Youth Talent Program

At IETF 125 in Shenzhen, approximately 60 students participated through the Chinese Youth Talent Program (CYTP)[Note 1] , representing more than 30 universities across China. This was not only an academic milestone for these young students but also a testament to the Chinese community’s growing engagement in the international arena of internet standards. This initiative marked a notable surge in regional student involvement, providing many first-time participants with direct exposure to the IETF process.


### Bridging the Academia with International Standards
A primary objective of CYTP is to promote the integration of cutting-edge academic innovation with international standards. Since the essence of the Internet lies in open collaboration and consensus-building, it is vital for young researchers to experience these dynamics firsthand. However, the transition from theoretical research to active standard-setting often involves significant barriers to entry. The CYTP aims to bridge this gap by providing Chinese university students with the necessary funding and mentorship to navigate and contribute to the IETF community.

### Supporting New IETF Participants
Specifically, the CYTP is intended to help students get involved in Internet standards work by lowering some of the practical barriers to participation, including travel support and guidance. It complements the IETF’s existing newcomer activities while also providing additional support before and during the meeting.

Alongside the usual IETF newcomer sessions, an “Ask Me Anything” discussion was organized to help students better understand how the IETF works in practice—how discussions happen, how to participate, and what to expect during working group meetings.


 


### Getting involved Before the IETF Week
Before the IETF125, several activities took place in order to make a selection of the students that can participate in the Chinese Youth Talent Program:

•	Deep-Dive Training: On 8 May 2025 the "IETF Standards Culture and Process Deep-Dive Training“ was hosted by CNNIC, the key sponsor of the IETF125, with support from Huawei. More than 150 professionals from operators, equipment manufacturers, internet companies, research institutions, and universities, attended this event. Two of the key goals of this event were: the (1) improvement of knowledge and understanding of IETF workflow and standardization process for employees of Chinese companies and University students and (2) support their engagement in IETF participation and in IETF standardization activities.

•	CYTP establishment and students selection: After the "IETF Standards Culture and Process Deep-Dive Training“ took place, activities started for the initiation of the CYTP, and the selection of the students that could attend the IETF 125 meeting. The steps that had been followed are: (1) promoted activity to find sponsors, (2) reached schools and professors in China and Malaysia, (3) survey on selecting students, i.e., from 80 candidates, selected 60 newcomer students to participate at IETF125.





### Getting Involved During the IETF Week
Students participated across a range of activities, including the Hackathon, working group (WG) sessions, and research group (RG) meetings. The topics they followed were quite broad—routing, transport, operations, security, and some of the newer discussions around AI-related work.

At the Hackathon, a number of students joined teams working on topics such as RPKI and networking-related use of machine learning. For many, this was their first exposure to the IETF’s “running code” approach, which left a strong impression.

During the main meeting, they attended sessions in groups like SPRING、IDR、DETNET、NMRG、SIDROPS、DTN、TIPTOP、CATS、SAVNET、V6OPS、MOPS、RTGWG、CCWG、DNSOP、GROW、DINRG、DMM、QUIC、TEAS and AIPREF. In addition, they also joined the hot AI Agent-related side meetings. In most cases, they started by observing, but some also asked questions and engaged in discussions. 

### What New Participants Asked (and Learned)
The “Ask Me Anything” session brought out a number of practical questions, including:

- How to get people to read and respond to an Internet-Draft?  
- How to understand which RFCs are actually used in practice?  
- Whether emerging areas like AI will change how the IETF works?  
- What it was like for experienced participants at their first IETF meeting?  

The answers were fairly consistent with what many in the community would recognize:

- Start by reviewing other people’s drafts;  
- Focus on specific, well-scoped problems; 
- Expect a learning curve, and don’t worry about not understanding everything at first.  

### First Impressions
Several students commented that the experience was quite different from what they expected. Mao Hongyao, an undergraduate student from Beijing Language and Culture University described it this way:

“I thought standards were something you design and then agree on. But I saw people spending a long time discussing the meaning of a single term. It felt less like ‘designing’ and more like something that evolves through discussion.”

That observation will probably resonate with many people who remember their first IETF meeting.

Another takeaway was that participation is a long-term process. A few students noted that even following discussions could be challenging at first, but that partial understanding is part of the process—and that familiarity builds over time.

### CYTP benefits
The successful implementation of the CYTP provides several benefits. For students, IETF 125 is a valuable international standard practice, allowing them to integrate into the global Internet core technology community, before graduation. For the IETF, this is a channel to stimulate the participation of newcomers. Young scholars are active and creative and can bring new ideas to IETF. They bring new solutions to the working groups with their academic perspective and thinking about real-world network problems. At the same time, the move has effectively extended the influence of the IETF in the Asia-Pacific region and attracted more Asian students and practitioners to participate in standard IETF activities.

### Beyond IETF 125
One observation that came up repeatedly is that a single meeting is only a starting point. Getting comfortable with the IETF takes time, and meaningful contributions usually come after sustained engagement—on mailing lists, in document reviews, and across multiple meetings.

For these students, IETF 125 was clearly an entry point. Whether that turns into longer-term participation will depend on how many of them stay involved after the meeting. CYTP is ready to support them. 

### Broadening Participation
From a community perspective, initiatives like CYTP can help bring in new participants, especially from regions where involvement has been more limited. At the same time, the experience at IETF 125 highlights that participation is not just about attending a meeting—it’s about continued engagement.

### Looking Ahead
The CYTP plans to continue supporting students at future IETF meetings. It will be interesting to see how many of the participants from IETF 125 remain active in the community over time.

We sincerely hope that the success of the CYTP will inspire other countries and regions around the world to launch similar projects. Whether in Europe, the Americas, or Africa, we encourage academia to link with industry to build bridges to the IETF for young local talent. It is the common hope of the IETF community that young engineers and researchers from all over the world will join hands in shaping the future of the Internet. We are convinced that a more diverse, inclusive, and dynamic IETF will better address future technological challenges and lay the foundation for the steady and prosperous global Internet.



Note1: The Chinese Youth Talent Program (CYTP) is supported by a consortium of organizations including China Internet Network Information Center (CNNIC), Tsinghua University, Internet Society of China, China Communications Standards Association (CCSA), Huawei Technologies Co., Ltd and ZTE Corporation.
